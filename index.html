The Problem

"I know I'm qualified —
but I freeze the moment the interview starts."

For non-native English speakers entering the US job market, interviews are a double barrier. The language gap isn't just vocabulary — it's the inability to practice in realistic, high-stakes conditions.

Existing options are either too expensive (professional career coaches at $150+/hr) or too shallow (AI bots with no real human connection). Nothing in between.

"I kept getting interviews. I kept getting ghosted. I couldn't tell if my English was the problem, or if I just didn't know how to interview."
— The question that started this project
~1M
international students in the US entering the job market each year
$150
average cost of a single professional career coaching session
0
affordable two-sided marketplace bridging language + cultural gap

Opportunity

A peer-to-peer platform where industry professionals offer affordable mock interviews and job seekers get real practice — with structured feedback.

◆ Discover — Research

Understanding the people
behind the problem

This project started from personal experience — I was the user I was designing for. After applying extensively, landing interviews, and repeatedly failing without understanding why, I started paying attention to the same pattern in peers around me.

I grounded the design in my own lived experience, informal conversations with others in the same situation, and a structured competitive analysis of existing platforms.

"I've sent over 300 applications. I get some interviews — but I don't know what I'm doing wrong in them. Nobody tells you."
— Observed pattern among non-native speakers in US job market
"I'd love to help people going through what I went through. I just have no platform to do it on."
— Observed pattern among professionals with immigrant backgrounds

◆ Discover — Key Findings

3 patterns that
kept coming up

01

Affordability is the #1 blocker

Cost came up consistently across peer conversations. Professional coaching is seen as a luxury, not a tool.

Consistently the first barrier mentioned — professional coaching is treated as a luxury.

02

Feedback quality is more important than practice volume

People don't want to repeat sessions — they want to know specifically what to fix. Generic feedback ("you did great!") is actively unhelpful and hard to act on.

Knowing what's wrong matters more than just doing it again.

03

Cultural fluency matters as much as English fluency

Many people have strong English skills but struggle with US workplace norms — directness, self-promotion, "Tell me about yourself." It's not just language.

The gap is behavioral and cultural, not just linguistic.

"Getting an interview is so hard right now — when you actually get one, you need to treat it like you can't afford to fail."
— Peer conversation, master's student entering US tech
"It's not just language. It's learning to brag. In my culture, you don't talk about yourself like that."
— A recurring theme in peer conversations

◆ Define — Problem Statement

Defined Problem Statement

Non-native English speakers in the US job market need an affordable, structured way to practice real interviews with experienced professionals — because the existing options are either too expensive, too shallow, or not culturally relevant.

How Might We…

make professional mock interviews affordable without sacrificing quality or human connection?
give interviewees feedback that is specific, culturally-aware, and actually actionable?
motivate experienced professionals to become mentors without adding friction to their schedule?

◆ Develop — Wireframes & Flow

From sketches to
structured screens

I started with hand sketches to explore rough layout ideas, then moved to low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. The key constraint was a mobile-first, single-screen-at-a-time architecture with a 5-tab bottom nav.

The wireframes cover 53 screens organized into 10 flow groups (A–J prefix system): auth, onboarding survey, home, profile, interview tracker, booking, meeting, transcript, and recording. The booking flow alone spans 14 screens — from mentor discovery through scheduling, payment, and post-session review.

◆ Develop — Design Decisions

Why I chose this
over that

Before
Session booking: 5-step form (details, schedule, type, payment, confirm)

Too many steps before seeing any value. Too much commitment too early.

→
After
3-step flow: pick a slot → add context → pay. Confirm in one screen.

Reduced perceived friction; most detail moved to post-session.

Before
Feedback form: open text field after each session

Open fields invite skipping. Users get nothing actionable.

→
After
Structured debrief: 5 sliders + mandatory strengths/improvement fields

Mentors fill it out faster. Users get structured, comparable feedback.

Before
In-app video room: auto-generated Daily.co link + Session Recording + View Transcript

Edge functions to create rooms, webhook for transcripts, recording storage pipeline — I had all of it built.

→
After
Expert pastes their own Teams / Zoom / Meet link when accepting

Zero infrastructure. Same UX outcome. The app's value is matching + coaching quality, not video tech.

I realized mid-build that I was constructing what amounts to a separate product — essentially Zoom. Cutting it was the right call.

Reflection & Next Steps

What I learned

The two-sided marketplace problem is real — and hard

Designing for two distinct user types (job seekers + expert mentors) with conflicting needs taught me to keep personas separate. A feature that reduces friction for one side often adds friction for the other. Every decision required a tradeoff negotiation.

Research is never "done enough"

My persona is still evolving. The mentor-side persona needs significantly more depth than I could reach through peer conversations alone. I've marked ongoing research explicitly in the prototype rather than pretending it's complete.

Knowing when to stop building is a design skill

At one point, the prototype had a full in-app video room: auto-generated meeting links via Daily.co, session recording, and a live transcript pipeline. I had built all of it. Then I realized I was constructing what amounts to Zoom — a separate product entirely. I replaced the whole system with one input field: the expert pastes their own Teams or Zoom link. Same UX outcome, zero infrastructure. The hardest part wasn't the technical work — it was recognizing that the work wasn't worth doing.

What I'd do differently

I'd start with user testing earlier — ideally at the wireframe stage, before any visual polish. I got attached to certain design decisions before validating them, which cost time during the iteration phase.

What's next

Usability testing with 5 target users (recruiting in progress)

Deeper mentor-side research and persona refinement

Accessibility audit and WCAG 2.1 AA compliance pass

Mobile performance optimization and real backend integration
